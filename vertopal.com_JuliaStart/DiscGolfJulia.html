A Look at the PDGA Worlds

In golf, and in disc golf, the day before the final round is called "moving day". The idea is that, by the end of the round, a player wants to be within striking distance of the lead for the final round of play, where they can then give more effort and give their all in order to win. The goal of this analysis is to observe and compare a player's ranking after each round to see when "moving" is ost effectively achieved.

Data Preparation

Table Scraping

The data that will be used for this project is not readily available for download for manipulation. To get the information needed for analysis, the tables were scraped and placed in a dataframe, then manipulated. An example of how the tables were scraped is in the code below.

The table below is from the 2017 PDGA World Championships. The world championships that were used for analysis were from 2018 to 2022. It was limited to this scope as the PDGA World Championship has changed formats from 4 rounds, a semi-final where the field is scraped, and then a final round between the top four to four rounds with a final round with the field scraped to only include the top players. In 2017, there were only four rounds played. The years used each had the same format, making it easier for analysis.

The library that was used for table scraping was TableScraper.jl. The function used (scrape_tables) returns an array of arrays—an array for each table found on the page containing seperate arrays for each row and an array for the table's headers. The initial full array is saved to "tab" below, which is then filtered down to just the "Male Pro Open" via tab[2]. This method of scraping returns data in type any. If the desired type is a string, it needs to be declared when the columns are created in the dataframe, and for Int and Float types, this can be done through parsing after the scraped data is pushed to the dataframe.

Data Manipulation

Before the analysis of the data can begin, the data needs to be manipulated to include the information we need. A file was scraped for each year between 2018 and 2022 and is read into an individual space first. The manipulation is done year by year, which could have more easily been done in a loop, but is taken individually for the time being for the purposes that: 1. this allows me to see the process gone through for each file and 2. the for loop in Julia is still being learned and can easily messed up with this many steps of manipulation.

Firstly, a year column was added to each dataframe so that when they are combined, they can still be differentiated. From there, each player's rank through each round was found by summing their scores through the round and then using the competerank function. The rank then allows us to see the movement of each player between rounds—where they were before the round compared to where they were ranked after the round was played (negative movement means dropping ranks). The movement allows us to analyze how each rank in each round impacts performance.

The plots that will be looked at loo at their rank at the beginning of the round compared to their rank movement due to their performance in the round. A look at round 1 sees the movement follow a linear movement with a large slope, meaning high volatility in rankings between rounds one and two. This signifies that performance in the first round can be easily negated by as early as round two (e.g. a player placing high in round 1 has as much a chance of being middle or even bottom of the pack after round one as keeping their place and a player near the bottom can still be near the top after round two).

Important to note, however, are the clusters that are seen around zero. Zero movement means keeping the place held at the beginning of the round. Clusters can be seen in the top ten and the bottom ten around zero. This could signify a top player going into the round with confidence and bottom player going into the round feeling defeated. Despite these clusters, the magnitude of change gets higher as rank gets higher.

The full analysis can fully be summarized in the table above. For every round, over 50% of the field is within ten places where they were in the previous round, including three rounds where over 65% of the field is within 10 places. The full movement, by comparison, only has around 39% of the field within ten places. This shows that there are no rounds of movement and no round that can be seen as a "moving day", but that the tournament as a whole is a war of attrition
